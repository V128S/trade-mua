You can't put a 3000+ W ASIC running at 75 dB in an apartment. But in 2026, manufacturers ship compact "home" miners with low power draw and acceptable noise that you can keep in a room or garage. Let's break down what home mining is, which models suit it, and whether you can heat your home with ASIC waste heat.

## How a home ASIC differs from an industrial one

| Parameter | Home ASIC | Industrial ASIC |
|---|---|---|
| Power | 100–1700 W | 3000–11000 W |
| Noise level | 35–50 dB | 70–80 dB |
| Hashrate | 6–90 TH/s | 200–1160 TH/s |
| Where to run | Room, garage | Data center, hotel |
| Power supply | Standard outlet | Industrial |

Home models trade absolute hashrate for quietness, low consumption, and the ability to run off a household outlet. They're a tool for those who want to enter mining without a dedicated room or an industrial tariff.

## Heating your home with miner waste heat

An ASIC converts almost all the electricity it draws into heat. In winter you can use this: the miner works as an electric heater that also earns income in coin. The logic is simple — you'd pay for heating anyway, and here the same kilowatt gives both heat and hashrate.

Caveats:
- The heat is dry and even, but you need ventilation to dump the excess in summer.
- Even a "quiet" miner makes background noise — it won't work in a bedroom.
- The economics only work where electricity is cheaper than gas/district heating, so calculate against your tariff.

## How much you can earn at home

Home mining isn't about getting rich quick. Avalon Q income is comparable to a few dollars a day before electricity, and it depends heavily on coin price and your [electricity tariff](/blog/electricity-cost-mining-ukraine-2026). On a residential tariff the margin is thin, so home mining pays off best:

1. with night (multi-zone) metering;
2. when the miner's heat replaces heating;
3. on efficient compact models.
